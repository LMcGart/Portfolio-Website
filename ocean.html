Ocean Keyboard

A unique custom mechanical keyboard kit designed to be the ideal combination of affordability, quality, and aesthetics.

May 2022 - Present

Project Description

The Ocean Keyboard was an extension of my business, Ocean Keys, which I started over the pandemic. Originally, I was importing mechanical keyboard parts and selling them to customers all across the United States. Over time I got more involved in the mechanical keyboard community and decided to start designing and building my own keyboard to fill a gap in the available product supply.

The Problem

When I started this project, the custom mechanical keyboard scene was very hard to get into. It was extremely expensive with budget keyboard options starting at around $250 fully built. Parts were also hard to source and the build process was confusing to beginners. Also, the budget keyboard options were either very plasticky or used a PCB mounting system that was not optimal, creating lackluster keystroke sounds, one of the most sought after qualities from hobbyists. My end goal with the Ocean Keyboard was to make a keyboard kit that was accessible and affordable for all. As I'll talk about further below, I started out creating the Ocean Keyboard using stacked pieces of lasercut acrylic. After designing and going through a few iterations of this first prototype, I began working on a version of the keyboard that was built using an SLA 3D printer.

Proposed Solution

At the time of building, most custom keyboards utilized a clamshell design where the top and bottom cases would be tightened together using screws. Even many of the 3D printed or acrylic keyboards used screws, potentially leading to thread stripping issues that would ruin the keyboard. The Ocean Keyboard introduces a first-of-kind magnetic mounting system that connects the top and bottom cases together using magnets. This negates potential threading issues, makes parts easily replaceable, and creates an easy and approachable build process. Additionally, using an SLA 3D printer creates an affordable keyboard case with a high quality finish.

The Designs

The Ocean Keyboard started off with a layered acrylic sheet construction. I designed the layers using a 2D software, Inkscape, and cut them out at a local makerspace using a laser cutter. I went through many design iterations in order to get the tolerances and alignment correct. When I got everything right, I glued the layers together and everything worked properly and the keyboard sounded great. However, gluing all of the layers together was not a scaleable process, and as I wanted to bring it to the market eventually, I had to think of another way to do it. I tried heat-set inserts and screws to keep the layers together but it warped the acrylic. To create a more premium product, I explored SLA 3D printing and found that to be the direction I wanted to go in.

SLA printing would allow me to create a top and bottom case that were each one solid piece, unlike the many layered acrylic prototype I previously created. To create the product I desired, I learned CAD using Autodesk Fusion 360. I spent some time iterating the design and making sure the dimensions and tolerances were correct and then I moved into the prototyping phase. The pictures above, as well as the first image on this page, are renders of the design I created. I wanted it to be a clean, simple, and elegant keyboard in order to appeal to as large of an audience as possible.

I began prototyping using an Anycubic Photon M3 Max but as seen above, the results were far from satisfactory. I went back to my designs and iterated them quite a few more times but I was still unable to create a product that I was satisfied with. Throughout the process I was reminded that things rarely ever go right on the first try. At this point, I had to go back to school and could not bring the printer with me so the project was put on pause.

Takeaways

While I was initially disappointed and felt that the whole project was a waste of time, money and resources, I realized it was an enriching learning experience. I gained a significant amount of knowledge covering 2D design software, CAD, laser cutters, and 3D printers. Perhaps my biggest learning was that going from a viable design to a usable product is a very long and complicated process that requires many iterations and adjustments to get right. I still believe my design is scaleable and I will continue to work on the keyboard and finish this project.
